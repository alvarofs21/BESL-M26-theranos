Case Overview

Theranos was founded in 2003 by 19-year-old Stanford dropout Elizabeth Holmes, who claimed to have developed a revolutionary blood-testing device called the Edison, capable of running over 200 diagnostic tests from a single finger-prick drop of blood. The promise was seductive: painless, fast, affordable testing available at your local pharmacy.

For over a decade, the company operated in near-total secrecy, raising nearly a billion dollars from prominent investors and signing major retail partnerships with Walgreens and Safeway. Holmes became the youngest self-made female billionaire in history, yet not a single product actually worked.

"Innovation without verification is not leadership. It is unmanaged risk."

The fraud unravelled in 2015, when Wall Street Journal investigative journalist John Carreyrou published a series of exposés revealing that Theranos was secretly running most of its tests on conventional competitor machines, and that its own Edison device produced dangerously inaccurate results. CMS inspections found "immediate jeopardy to patient safety." By 2018, the company had dissolved. In 2022, Holmes was convicted on four counts of fraud and sentenced to over 11 years in federal prison.

What makes Theranos more than a corporate scandal is the scale of systemic failure surrounding it: regulators, investors, boards, partners, and the media all failed simultaneously, while hundreds of thousands of real patients were given false diagnoses.

02

Key Insights

Insight 01

Governance Without Expertise is Decoration

Theranos' board included two former Secretaries of State, a former Secretary of Defense, and a former Senator. Not a single doctor, scientist, or diagnostics expert sat at that table. Prestige replaced scrutiny. Fast capital and prestigious names mean nothing if nobody in the room can evaluate the core claim.

Insight 02

Healthcare Fraud Has Compound Consequences

Unlike financial fraud, the cost of deception in healthcare spreads to patients, public trust, and future innovators. The "Theranos effect" chilled legitimate health-tech investment for years, punishing the industry long after the company dissolved.

Insight 03

Culture is the Real Test of Leadership

If dissent is punished rather than protected, the organisation is not innovative. It is fragile. Theranos systematically silenced whistleblowers through legal intimidation and deliberate information siloing. The strongest cultures are those where bad news travels fastest.

Insight 04

Journalism as the Last Line of Defence

When regulators, investors, and boards all failed simultaneously, a single journalist, John Carreyrou, did what billions of dollars of governance could not. His WSJ reporting triggered the regulatory scrutiny, partner exits, and criminal investigation that followed.

Ethical Leadership

Authentic vs. Performative Leadership

Ethical leadership theory distinguishes between leaders whose values are genuine versus those who perform authenticity as strategy. Holmes exemplifies performative leadership: the persona was engineered to replace evidence with emotion. Avolio & Gardner (2005) argue authentic leaders build trust through transparency; Holmes did the opposite, using conviction to substitute for proof.

The board had more military stars than scientific credentials

Agency Theory

The Principal-Agent Problem in Governance

Agency theory (Jensen & Meckling, 1976) describes how principals (boards) struggle to monitor agents (management) without domain expertise. At Theranos, the board lacked the technical capacity to detect deception. Information asymmetry shifted entirely to Holmes. Prestige does not compensate for the absence of oversight; without it, the monitoring function collapses.

In-Group Loyalty and Dissent Suppression

Janis's groupthink model (1972) identifies how cohesive groups suppress dissent to maintain shared identity. George Shultz's loyalty to Holmes over his own grandson shows how powerful in-group dynamics override individual judgment. The social cost of dissent, including family rupture, mirrors the organisational cost Theranos imposed on employees who questioned the technology.

Curiosity 04

A regulatory loophole made the fraud easier to hide

Theranos exploited the Laboratory Developed Test (LDT) classification to avoid FDA oversight for over 12 years, a known regulatory blind spot that this case directly catalysed debate about closing.

Agency Theory

Regulatory Capture and Information Asymmetry

Agency theory extends to regulators as agents of the public interest. When regulators lack technical capacity, or when firms exploit classification loopholes, information asymmetry shifts decisively toward the regulated party. Theranos operated 12+ years without a mandatory technical audit, showing how regulatory gaps become structural enablers of fraud.

Curiosity 05

Employees were kept deliberately in the dark

Theranos ran in strict information silos, where most employees had no visibility beyond their own team. It was framed as protecting IP, but it was the actual mechanism that prevented anyone from seeing the full picture of the fraud.

Groupthink & Cognitive Bias

Structural Silencing and the Illusion of Normalcy

Information siloing is a structural form of groupthink management. Limiting what individuals can see removes the raw material needed for dissent. Employees experience a manufactured version of reality that appears normal. Festinger's cognitive dissonance theory applies: without contradicting evidence, people rationalise anomalies rather than challenge the system producing them.

Ethical Leadership

Dual Roles and Conflicts of Interest

Ethical leadership frameworks emphasise role clarity and separation of personal and professional relationships as safeguards. The Holmes-Balwani dynamic concentrated power in two people with fully aligned personal incentives, effectively eliminating internal checks. Brown & Treviño (2006) define ethical leaders as those who model appropriate conduct through their relationships; deliberate concealment from the board is a textbook failure of that standard.

Discussion Questions

Groupthink · Cognitive Bias

Most people, placed in the room with Holmes in 2013, would have said yes, and that discomfort is exactly the point. The social proof cascade (a board including Kissinger and Shultz, $900M raised, Walgreens as a partner) created powerful availability bias: if this many credible people believed it, trust felt more rational than scrutiny.

The harder questions would have required resisting the narrative pull and demanding independent technical validation from a domain expert, not a prestige signal. The lesson: the strength of a story should increase your burden of proof, not reduce it.

Agency Theory · Ethical Leadership

Holmes bears primary moral and legal responsibility as the architect of the deception. But agency theory makes clear that the board failed their monitoring duty; investors funded vision without verification; and regulators allowed a structural loophole to persist for over a decade. Responsibility is distributed, not singular.

The more powerful framing for future leaders: you will likely sit on the investor or governance side of this equation, not the Holmes side. The question is whether you would ask the questions that nobody else did.

Ethical Leadership · Stakeholder Theory

Legitimate IP protection means not disclosing proprietary processes to competitors. It does not mean concealing whether a product functions at all. The ethical line is crossed when secrecy prevents stakeholders from making informed decisions that affect their own wellbeing.

Freeman's stakeholder theory (1984) is useful here: firms have obligations to all parties materially affected by their actions. When Theranos' secrecy put patient health at risk, it had already moved from competitive strategy to ethical violation, regardless of whether any law had been technically broken yet.

Business Environment · Groupthink

The "fake it till you make it" norm is specific to Silicon Valley's growth-at-all-costs culture and it works in software, where shipping a buggy product has limited immediate harm. Applied to healthcare diagnostics, the same norm becomes catastrophic. Sector context shaped investor expectations, board composition, and regulatory assumptions simultaneously.

The broader lesson: industry culture is itself a governance variable. As future managers, the question is not just "is this legal?" but "does this environment make rational oversight structurally harder?" If yes, your individual burden of vigilance increases accordingly.
